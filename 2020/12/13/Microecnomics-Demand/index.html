Microeconomics let’s talk about demand

Individual demand without influnce from others

Under the assumption that individuals can decide their own demand within a budget, I’d like to explain what a demand curve is.

This chapter is all about how to find out the demand curve of an individual while varying its budget and the type of the goods that’s consumed.

We devide goods into two types

Type 1: Ordinary goods

These are goods that we will purchase more if our budget goes up, and less if our budget goes down.

Type 2: Giffen goods

These are goods that we will purchase less if our budget goes up, yet we will buy more of them if our budget goes down.

An example would be bread or rice which is the main source of nourishment of most people. If the price of it goes up, since everything else is still as expensive, we have no choice but to give up our budget for other things (e.g meat/fish etc.) to buy more bread or rice.

Law of demand

We use the following formula to characterize the essence of ordianry and giffen goods:

ordinary goods

For ordinary goods: $\frac{dq_i}{dp_i}<0$ with $q_i:=\text{quantity of a good i}$ and $p_i:=\text{price of a good i}$

This inequation says that when the price of a good i goes up, it’s consumed quantity is going to go down, hence resulting in a negative change in quantity with the increase of price.

giffen goods

For giffen goods: $\frac{dq_i}{dp_i}>0$ with $q_i:=\text{quantity of a good i}$ and $p_i:=\text{price of a good i}$

This inequation states the exact opposite to the definition of ordinary goods.

When the price of a giffen goods goes up, the change in quantity (which is in this case related to the price) is going to be greater than zero, hence postive.

Demand curve

Assumed that we know

  1. our budget m
  2. the price of good 1
  3. the price of good 2

Furthermore, assume that we have a specific good 1, say hamburgers, that we would want to consume.

First and foremost, we should know that each point on the demand curve shows up the optimal consumption amount of hamburgers under its given price and income, other than that it can tell us what our maximum willingness to pay for a given quantity is. Meaning, if I am offered x hamburgers, how much am I willing to pay for those.

How to find out the demand curve

Assume we have an intitial budget m = 20 and the price of hamburgers = 2, price of other goods =2.

We will vary the price of hamburgers throughout the process

The demand curve shows the relation between the price and the desired amount of one particular good, so we will draw another graph that plots the optimal consumption amount of hamburgers and at which price we purchase these burgers.



Again, each point on the demand curve shows us the optimal consumption amount at a particular price within a budget (e.g at p=0.5, #hamburgers=20). Why is that? Because we have calculated the optimal consumption amount in the first place.

This also tells us what our maximum willingness to pay for a certain amount of the particular good is. This, we call the WTP, which can also be referred to as reservation price. That means, that we will never pay more than 0.5 when we are offered 20 burgers.

Price Elasticity of demand

After learning how to find out the demand curve, we want to know how the demand Q is related to a change in price P.

Putting the example above in this context, we will want to find out how the demand for burgers changes according to its price changes.

$\eta_P(Q)=\frac{\triangle Q}{\triangle P}\frac{P}{Q}=\frac{d Q}{d P}\frac{P}{Q}$

The above equation expresses our quest to find out the changes in demand Q relating to the changes in price P.

To calculate that, we need to, according to the equation, get the first derivative of the demand function of the variable P. That being said, we will need a function which looks like this:

$Q=…$

Say we have a demand equation $Q=10-2P$, then our price elasticity is going to be $\eta_P=Q’\frac{P}{Q}=2P\frac{P}{Q}$.

Depending on what our $P$ is, the elasticity will be calculated, and the result will be in one of these 3 ranges:

  1. $|\eta_P|<1$
  2. $|\eta_P|=1$
  3. $|\eta_P|>1$

The first elasticity is described as inelastic, that means, if we have a 1% change in price, there will be a change less than 1% in our demand, which is rather small and hence inelastic.

Respectively, we call a change elastic when it demonstrates a greater than 1% change when the price renders a 1% change.

The change = 1% is called unit elastic. Which means that when the price increases, the expenditure stays the same.

Remember that these definitions refer to the absolute value of the price elasticity!

Extreme cases for price elasticity

Perfectly elastic demand

In this case, I will have a commodity which I will only consume at a particular price, meaning that I will switch to other subsitutes if the price of this comodity or good increases.

The graph shows a straight line that only intersects with the y-axis in one exact point, with y-axis plotting the price and x-axis the amount of consumed goods.

$\eta_P=-\infty$

Perectly inelastic demand

This good that has perfect inelaticity is something of necessity and has no substitues. (e.g Water in dessert)

I will always purchase it at a particular amount no matter what price it’s at.

The graph will show a straight horizontal line that intersects only with the x-axis (when it plots the demand and y-axis the price) in one point.

Demand Curve and Other Prices

The assumption that we have been using so far is that we have exactly the same budget and the same price for other goods.

Changes in price for other goods

Consider this to be the new assumption and we no longer change the price of the good whose demand we want to observe.

Say we want to observe the change in demand for good 1 and good 2 is something we also consume. We want to change the price of good 2 under a given budget to see how this will affect our demand for good 1.

To find that out, we need to know that type of goods we are talking about.

Complement

When good 1 und good 2 are complement to each other, we will always want to consume them together (e.g socks).

As P2 (price of good 2) increases, optimal consumption Q1 decrease because I will not be able to afford the same amount of good 2 due to the increase in P2 and I will not want to consume the same amount of good 2 either.

$\eta_{P2}(Q1)<0$

Substitute

When good 1 is the substitue for good 2, we will be consuming more good 1 when the price of good 2 goes up.

$\eta_{P2}(Q1)>0$

Changes in income

To find out what the changes in income do to our demand for a particular good 1, we will categorize all goods into

Inferior Good

As our budget m goes up, we will demand less of it.

$\eta_m(Q1)<0$

In this case, the demand curve shifts to the left.

Why?

As our budget increases, we will have a budget line that moves rightward, and the point that marks the consumption will shifts inward (nearing the y-axis).

Normal Good

As the budget m goes up, we will consume more of it.

$\eta_m(Q1)>0$

The budget line shifts rightward, so does our demand curve.

Aggregate Demand / Market Demand

Aggregated deman = market deman = sum up every point of the individual deman curves

Networt Externalities

We have been assuming that we are the only ones who decide our demand for goods. But some goods underly the effect of the so-called network externalities.

When the effect is positive, that’s called

Bandwagon Effect

This is an effect that has an impact on our demand for one good and this is positive as in it will increase our demand for that good.

It can be resulted by technological positive externalities (social Networks, Software, Telephone, Email etc.) and psychological positive externalities (clothes—fashion trend, music etc.)

When this effect takes place, our demand is going to increase when the price decreases. We can observe that this demand is more elastic. (As the price changes, the demand will change in a greater proportion)

Say that we have a piece of clothing which underlies no bandwagon effect. When the price falls from 100 dollars to 50, its consumption amount (demand) will increase from 50 to 100. When the bandwagon effect kicks in, we will see a significant growth in demand.

When the effect does not increase the demand when the price goes down, this is called the

Snob Effect

Reasons can be technological negative externalities (e.g beaches, more people less attractive, the same goes for ski resorts etc.).

Not that one will always observe a negative growth in demand, it will certainly be a more inelastic demand.
